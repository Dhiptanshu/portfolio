A great vision gets a startup off the ground, but a solid legal foundation and airtight financials keep it alive.

For Day 8 of the PIERC - Parul University Cohort 9 Startup Pre-Incubation Program, my friend Kavya Joshi and I visited the Vadodara Startup Studio, PIERC. Established by PIERC - Parul University in 2021, this space offers resources and mentorship for aspiring entrepreneurs. Representing the Faculty of Engineering and Technology GLS University, GLS University here felt like stepping into the operational heart of the startup ecosystem.

We started with Legal Structures and Compliance led by CS Prachi Lad. She emphasized that early compliance prevents future legal messes. We explored Sole Proprietorships, Partnerships, Limited Liability Partnerships (LLPs), and Companies, including One Person Company (OPC), Private Limited, and Public Limited, alongside Hindu Undivided Family (HUF), Section 8 Companies, Society, Trusts, and NGOs. A major takeaway was why investors prefer a Private Limited Company over an LLP. It comes down to trust. Companies require strict audits and exact Meeting Minutes during Director Meetings, giving investors immense confidence.

Next, Mr. HARDIK KHARVA broke down Cap Tables, Valuation, and Deal Structuring. A Cap (Capitalization) Table shows who owns what percentage of a company at a given time. We learned about equity dilution when new investors buy in. For valuation, we discussed the Revenue Multiple Method using benchmarks from platforms like Tracxn. If a startup has no revenue, a Compulsorily Convertible Debenture (CCD) is used. It acts like a loan that turns into shares later, using a Valuation Floor and Cap to protect both parties.

Mr. HARDIK KHARVA also covered Unit Economics and Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC). A Unit is the single item you measure, such as a customer, product, or transaction. We looked at formulas involving Average Order Value (AOV) and Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) to calculate Gross Margin. We explored Blended CAC, the total average cost to acquire a customer across all channels, and the crucial LTV to CAC Ratio. This compares a customer's Lifetime Value (LTV) against the cost to acquire them. If your LTV is not significantly higher than your CAC, the business model might not be sustainable.

Understanding the legal and financial reality is what separates a passionate idea from a sustainable company. We left the studio today with a concrete roadmap to build something that lasts.

P.S. Thank you to Mr. Bhavya Patel for his constant support and mentorship.

We are exactly one week into the PIERC - Parul University Startup Pre-Incubation Program at Parul University, and Day 7 shifted our focus entirely. Today was about how to sustain, brand, and sell our project.

We started the morning by watching the Post-Budget Webinar for 2026 and 2027, focusing on 'Sabka Saath, Sabka Vikas'. Hearing insights from the Prime Minister and government leadership about the future of innovation in India gave us a strong sense of purpose before starting our core sessions.

The most inspiring part of the day was Mr. Shlok Solanki discussing Sales Strategy. He completely changed the way we look at selling. He explained that every customer has a CS, which stands for Current Situation, and a DS, which stands for Desired Situation. The goal of sales is simply to position your product as the bridge that fills the gap between the two. If you can clearly show how your product takes them from their current problem to their desired goal, the customer will buy it.

He also taught us that sales is all about repetitive effort. The more you try, the more success you will see. As founders, we have to grow a thick skin and learn to handle rejections gracefully. He introduced a fascinating concept called Respected Broke versus Rejected Millionaire. This means it is better to face rejection repeatedly and build a successful, wealthy business than to protect your pride, avoid rejection entirely, and stay broke. He also reminded us that boring means money, meaning the most successful businesses often just solve practical, everyday problems.

The day ended with a big moment for us. My friend Kavya Joshi and I presented our startup idea, focused on our Value Proposition, Business Model, and Market Survey & Research. Pitching our vision after a full day of learning about funding, branding, and sales felt incredibly grounding. We are clearer than ever on our direction.

Thank you to all the speakers today, and to the Faculty of Engineering and Technology GLS University, GLS University for the constant support on this journey. We are not just building a product. We are building for impact.

Following up on our deep dive into value propositions yesterday, Day 4 of the PIERC - Parul University Cohort 9.0 Startup Pre-Incubation Program at Parul University focused on understanding the battlefield. Today, we tackled Market Research and Competitor Analysis. I was with Kavya Joshi representing the Faculty of Engineering and Technology GLS University, GLS University.

We started by breaking down Market Research into four main pillars:

Customer Discovery

Market Sizing. This involves understanding our TAM, SAM, and SOM. This measures total universal demand, the specific portion we can reach, and the realistic slice we can capture right now.

Competitive Analysis. This means looking at Direct competitors doing the exact same thing, Indirect competitors solving the same problem differently, and Emerging competitors just entering the space.

Pricing Strategy to maximize revenue.

From there, we went deeper into market segmentation. We learned to find our specific niche using primary research (talking directly to potential customers) and secondary research (studying existing industry data). We also learned to spot common research mistakes early and detect potential challenges before they become roadblocks.

One of the most engaging parts of the day was an interactive Mentimeter session focused on Competitor Analysis. It completely shifted our perspective on indirect competitors. For example, if you are launching a new airline, you might think your only competition is other airlines like Indigo or Air India. However, one of your biggest actual competitors is the Indian Railways. They solve the same customer need for travel, just in a different way. It was a great reminder to look outside the obvious categories.

We also covered the spectrum of Affordability versus Premium. This is the business decision of whether to offer a budget-friendly product to a mass market or a high-end product to a select few. Another key concept was top-down versus bottom-up market segmentation. Top-down starts with a massive view of the entire industry and narrows it down to your specific target audience. Bottom-up does the opposite. It starts with your local sales estimates and builds upward to project your overall market potential.
Finally, we spent time on the critical importance of real customer interaction to test business assumptions. We learned how to map out what we think we know through assumption mapping, and more importantly, how to validate those assumptions through actual market research using Hypothesis Testing and Hypothesis Learning cards.

It was an intense but incredibly valuable day of learning. We are leaving with a clearer picture of our market environment and the right tools to analyze it properly.

We are grateful to PIERC - Parul University for having us and to our mentors today - Bhavya Patel and Dr. Prashant Khanna (Ph.D), for their helpful insights. Next step - field research!!

Ending 2025 with a blast!! I am happy to share that I have just completed an online course on Blockchain Technology [90 hrs] by Reliance Foundation Skilling Academy through the Skill India Digital Hub and National Skill Development Corporation (NSDC).

Understanding Blockchain Technology in today's world is necessary. Through this interactive course, I explored the world of Blockchain and cryptocurrencies from a very fundamental level with practical exposure through activities. These activities allowed me to explore the architecture, working, and security of Blockchain, the working of Bitcoin, along with crucial Ethereum development technologies.

I also explored cryptocurrency mining, and validation via consensus mechanisms like Proof-of-Work (PoW) - where the miners solve complex mathematical problems to validate a transaction and add a block to the ledger, the more energy efficient Proof-of-Stake (PoS) - where the validators 'stake' a collateral amount to create new blocks, the less scalable multi-state voting algorithm Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance (PBFT) - where 2 out of 3 honest nodes confirm a state before moving to the next, and more.

Most importantly, I got the necessary practical exposure by exploring Cryptocurrency wallets like WazirX and MetaMask, Ethereum-based Decentralized Applications (DApps), and creating my own Smart Contracts. Smart Contracts are self-executing programs that act like agreements, automating the actions required in a blockchain transaction. Smart Contracts are faster, cost-efficient, transparent, and secure, as they are based on the decentralized blockchain technology.

I created my first smart contracts using the Remix IDE. This IDE allowed me to write contracts based on the Object-Oriented Programming Language - Solidity efficiently. After defining the compiler version in the code, we define a Contract, which acts like a Class in the program. The contract can contain several functions, each having a gas fee. Gas fees are like a transaction cost, which is paid in ETH to a validator on the Ethereum network. After I have finished writing my contract, I save the '.sol' file and compile it using a selected compiler version. After compiling, I can easily deploy it on an Ethereum network thanks to Remix IDE. I deployed my contracts on the Sepolia Testnet. A testnet is a parallel version of a LIVE blockchain network that is used for development with no real costs. I was also easily able to push it to my remote repository on GitHub using simple Git commands, which were built into the Remix IDE GUI.

This is just a start, as I am planning to go deeper into smart contracts and then go on to develop DApps. I am thankful to the Reliance Foundation and the National Skill Development Corporation for building and providing this fundamental and interactive course.
